Understanding the Challenge

The Space Environment Crisis

Space debris poses an increasing threat to the orbital environment. With millions of pieces of debris from past missions, launches, and collisions, the space around Earth is becoming increasingly congested, threatening future space activities and the satellites we depend on daily.

Critical Infrastructure at Risk

Modern society depends on space-based services including GPS navigation, weather forecasting, telecommunications, and Earth observation. Space debris threatens this critical infrastructure, potentially disrupting services that billions of people rely on every day.

The Need for Sustainable Practices

Sustainable space activities require international cooperation, responsible mission design, and effective debris mitigation strategies. The choices we make today will determine whether future generations can continue to benefit from space exploration and utilization.

Our Core Objectives

Education & Awareness

Advancing public understanding of space sustainability challenges through educational resources, research publications, and public outreach programs that explain the importance of responsible space activities.

International Cooperation

Fostering collaboration between nations, space agencies, commercial operators, and research institutions to develop coordinated approaches to space sustainability and debris mitigation.

Sustainable Space Practices

Promoting the adoption of sustainable spacecraft design principles, responsible mission planning, and effective end-of-life disposal strategies to minimize the creation of new space debris.

Our Mission

To advance space sustainability through research, education, and advocacy, ensuring that space remains accessible and safe for current and future generations. We work to preserve the orbital environment as a shared global resource while supporting continued exploration and utilization of space for peaceful purposes.

The Challenges We Face

The Space Debris Crisis

Earth's orbit contains over 130 million pieces of space debris larger than 1mm, with 34,000 objects larger than 10cm actively tracked. This debris travels at speeds up to 17,500 mph, making even small fragments potentially catastrophic to active satellites and spacecraft.

Key Fact: A collision with a piece of debris just 1cm in size can destroy a satellite.

The Kessler Syndrome

Named after NASA scientist Donald Kessler, this theoretical scenario describes a cascade effect where collisions create more debris, which causes more collisions, potentially making certain orbital regions unusable for decades.

Did You Know? We're already seeing early signs of Kessler Syndrome in Low Earth Orbit.

How Orbits Work

Low Earth Orbit (LEO)

Altitude: 160-2,000 km above Earth
Orbital Period: 90-120 minutes
Examples: ISS, most satellites, Starlink constellation

Challenge: Most crowded orbital region with highest debris density.

Geostationary Orbit (GEO)

Altitude: 35,786 km above Earth
Orbital Period: 24 hours (matches Earth's rotation)
Examples: Communication satellites, weather satellites

Challenge: Limited orbital slots, difficult to reach for cleanup missions.

Orbital Velocity

Objects in orbit must travel at precise speeds to maintain their altitude. In LEO, this means approximately 7.8 km/s (17,500 mph). Any change in velocity affects the orbit's shape and altitude.

Physics Fact: Speeding up actually lowers your orbit, slowing down raises it.

Industry Solutions in Development

Tracking and Prediction

Advanced radar and optical sensors monitoring space objects 24/7. AI algorithms to predict collision risks and alert satellite and spacecraft operators to perform emergency collision avoidance maneuvers.

How it works: Ground-based sensors track objects, machine learning predicts future positions, automated systems send collision warnings.

Active Debris Removal

Specialized missions capturing and removing large debris objects using robotic arms, nets, or harpoons. These missions aim to target the most dangerous pieces of debris that pose the highest collision risk.

Current Status: ESA's ClearSpace-1 mission will remove a rocket upper stage in 2025.

Space Traffic Management

Coordinated global systems managing spacecraft movements like air traffic control, preventing collisions through standardized protocols, orbital slot allocation, and real-time inter-agency coordination.

Future Vision: Automated collision avoidance, standardized communication protocols, and international orbital highways.

Key Concepts to Understand

Conjunction Events

When two objects in space are predicted to come within a few kilometers of each other. Operators must then decide whether to perform avoidance maneuvers based on collision probability calculations, and one mistake can be costly.

Threshold: Maneuvers typically considered when collision probability exceeds 1 in 10,000.

Post-Mission Disposal

International guidelines require satellites to be removed from useful orbits within 25 years of mission end, either by deorbiting or moving to a "graveyard orbit". However newer ESA missions are given a 5 year time frame instead.

Reality Check: Currently, only ~60% of missions comply with these guidelines.

Situational Awareness

The ability to detect, track, and predict the movement of objects in space. This includes active satellites, debris, and natural objects like comets and / or asteroids in an effort to reduce risk to future missions.

Global Effort: Multiple countries contribute tracking data to shared databases.
