*Establishing a Circular Economy for Earth's Orbital Environment*

## Executive Summary
Since the dawn of the Space Age, Earth orbit has become one of humanity's most valuable shared environments. Satellites now underpin global communications, navigation, weather forecasting, scientific research, disaster response, financial systems, agriculture, environmental monitoring and countless other services upon which modern society depends.

Alongside this remarkable expansion has come an increasingly significant environmental challenge. Thousands of inactive satellites, spent launch vehicle components and fragments of orbital debris now occupy Earth's orbital environment. Each object represents not only a collision risk to operational spacecraft, but also contributes to a growing cycle of orbital congestion that threatens the long-term sustainability of space activities.

Current international efforts focus primarily on debris mitigation, responsible mission design and the removal of selected high-risk debris objects. While these initiatives are essential, they largely address individual missions rather than establishing permanent infrastructure capable of maintaining Earth's orbital environment as a shared global resource.

The Orbital Conservation Institute proposes the Orbital Environmental and Sustainability Architecture (OESA) as a long-term framework for orbital stewardship. OESA envisions an internationally governed network of permanent Orbital Service Stations (OSS) supported by autonomous servicing vehicles, common engineering standards and a circular orbital economy in which satellites are routinely inspected, repaired, upgraded, recycled or responsibly decommissioned rather than abandoned.

Rather than viewing satellites as disposable assets, OESA encourages a future in which spacecraft are treated as maintainable infrastructure throughout their operational lifecycles.

This paper presents the guiding principles, proposed architecture and governance concepts intended to stimulate international discussion and collaboration.

## Introduction
Humanity stands at an important moment in the history of space exploration.

Earth orbit has evolved from an experimental frontier into a critical extension of global infrastructure. More than ever before, governments, commercial organisations, research institutions and emerging space nations rely upon satellites to support economic prosperity, scientific advancement and public safety.

As launch costs continue to decline and satellite constellations expand, the number of active spacecraft is expected to increase substantially over the coming decades. Without corresponding advances in orbital stewardship, this growth risks accelerating congestion and increasing the likelihood of collisions, debris generation and avoidable loss of valuable spacecraft.

On Earth, critical infrastructure is maintained through roads, ports, maintenance depots, recycling facilities and engineering standards. Ships are repaired rather than abandoned, aircraft undergo scheduled maintenance and industrial materials are increasingly recovered through circular economic models.

No equivalent permanent infrastructure currently exists in Earth orbit.

OESA seeks to address this absence by proposing a sustainable framework that enables the routine maintenance of the orbital environment through international cooperation.

## The Need for Orbital Stewardship
The sustainability of Earth's orbital environment cannot be achieved solely through responsible satellite design.

Long-term stewardship requires infrastructure capable of responding to spacecraft throughout their entire operational lifecycle.

Several challenges illustrate this need.

+ Ageing satellites frequently retain valuable structural components long after individual subsystems have failed.
+ Minor hardware failures often result in the premature loss of otherwise functional spacecraft.
+ Valuable materials launched from Earth remain inaccessible once satellites become inactive.
+ Existing debris continues to accumulate faster than it can be removed.
+ Future satellite constellations will place increasing pressure upon already congested orbital regions.

These challenges represent environmental, economic and operational concerns that extend beyond individual missions.

A sustainable orbital environment requires permanent engineering capability.

## Vision
The Orbital Environmental and Sustainability Architecture seeks to establish Earth orbit as a permanently maintained environment through peaceful international cooperation.

Its vision is simple:

> *"Earth orbit should be maintained, not abandoned."*

Just as terrestrial infrastructure enables the maintenance of cities, transport networks and oceans, orbital infrastructure should enable the maintenance of humanity's growing presence in space.

OESA therefore proposes a transition from a predominantly disposable satellite economy towards a circular orbital economy centred upon servicing, refurbishment, recycling and responsible retirement.

## Guiding Principles
### Environmental Responsibility
Earth orbit should be recognised as a shared environment requiring active protection for future generations.

### International Cooperation
Orbital sustainability should be pursued collaboratively through peaceful multinational participation.

### Circular Resource Management
Spacecraft and orbital materials should be recovered, reused and recycled wherever practical.

### Transparency
Engineering research conducted through OESA should be published openly for the benefit of the global scientific and engineering community, except where publication would compromise legitimate security or proprietary information.

### Neutrality
OESA infrastructure should remain politically neutral and operate solely in support of orbital sustainability objectives.

### Respect for Sovereignty
No satellite shall be serviced, repaired, inspected or recovered without the explicit authorisation of its lawful owner or operator. Military and other sensitive government spacecraft shall only be handled with the consent of the owning state and in accordance with internationally agreed procedures.


---
## The Orbital Environmental and Sustainability Architecture
OESA is proposed as an integrated framework rather than a single engineering project.

Its principal components include:

+ Orbital Service Stations (OSS)
+ Autonomous servicing and recovery drones
+ Orbital debris collection capability
+ Satellite repair facilities
+ Orbital recycling facilities
+ In-space manufacturing capability
+ Standardised satellite servicing interfaces
+ International governance mechanisms
+ Orbital Environmental Credits
+ Open engineering research programmes

Together, these elements establish a permanent ecosystem supporting the long-term sustainability of Earth's orbital environment.
